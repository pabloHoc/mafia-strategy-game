# Institutions

**Status:** WIP

## Core Idea

Institutions are major non-player actors that operate within the city.

Examples:

- Police
- Courts
- City Hall
- Press
- Unions
- Religious Organizations
- Harbor Authority

Institutions are not resources, assets, or NPC collections.

They are active forces that generate pressure, opportunities, and constraints for criminal organizations.

---

## Design Goals

- Make the city feel alive and reactive.
- Create external pressures beyond rival factions.
- Provide meaningful uses for leverage assets.
- Avoid managing dozens of individual NPCs.
- Avoid influence bars and reputation meters.

---

## What Institutions Are Not

### Not NPC Collections

Rejected approach:

- 5 politicians per district
- 3 judges per district
- 4 police officers per district

This creates large numbers of mostly inactive entities and management overhead.

---

### Not Influence Tracks

Rejected approach:

- Police Influence: 37
- Court Influence: 52
- Political Influence: 81

This tends to become another resource bar that players repeatedly fill and spend.

---

## Institution Behavior

Institutions should actively affect the game world.

Examples:

### Police

May:

- Investigate crimes
- Generate Heat pressure
- Raid assets
- Arrest agents

### Courts

May:

- Prosecute cases
- Imprison agents
- Resolve legal disputes

### Press

May:

- Expose scandals
- Increase public scrutiny
- Influence city sentiment

### Politics

May:

- Change city conditions
- Create opportunities
- Restrict criminal activity

---

## Leverage Assets

Institutions are intended to be manipulated through leverage.

Examples:

- Favor
- Debt
- Blackmail
- Bribe
- Political Support

These are not influence points.

They are tools used to alter or bypass institutional actions.

---

## Example Usage

Situation:

- A lieutenant is arrested.

Possible solutions:

- Spend a Police Favor.
- Spend Blackmail on law enforcement.
- Spend Wealth as a bribe.
- Spend Political Support.

The player chooses how to solve the problem based on available leverage.

## Additional Design Directions

### Institution Competition

Institutions are not always independent.

They may occasionally come into conflict with one another, creating indirect ways for players to manipulate outcomes.

Examples:

- Press exposes police corruption.
- Politicians pressure courts.
- Unions oppose city hall.
- Religious organizations oppose political initiatives.

This allows leverage to be applied through multiple channels rather than directly targeting a single institution.

Example:

Instead of bribing the police, the player leaks information to the press, creating pressure that weakens police actions.

**Purpose**

- Increase strategic options.
- Create indirect solutions.
- Make institutions feel connected rather than isolated systems.

---

### Institution Agendas

Institutions may have temporary priorities or concerns.

Examples:

Police:
- Gang violence.
- Smuggling.
- Corruption.

Press:
- Political scandal.
- Missing persons.
- Corruption investigations.

Courts:
- Organized crime prosecutions.
- Property disputes.

Agendas help explain why institutions are acting and provide context for events and pressures.

**Purpose**

- Create believable institutional behavior.
- Generate varied situations.
- Reduce reliance on random events.

---

### Interaction With District Axes

Institution activity may be influenced by district conditions.

Examples:

- High Order districts attract stronger law enforcement activity.
- High Wealth districts attract financial scrutiny.
- High Influence districts attract political attention.

This connects Institutions to existing district systems rather than creating a separate gameplay layer.

**Purpose**

- Increase systemic interaction.
- Make district development affect external pressures.
- Create emergent opportunities and risks.

---

### Institutions Generate Opportunities

Institutions should not only create threats.

Examples:

- Police investigate a rival faction.
- Election season creates political openings.
- Corruption scandals weaken competitors.
- Labor disputes disrupt enemy operations.

Players should sometimes benefit from institutional activity.

**Purpose**

- Prevent institutions from becoming pure punishment systems.
- Create opportunities for opportunistic play.
- Encourage players to pay attention to institutional events.

---

### Multi-Purpose Leverage

Leverage assets should have multiple valid uses.

Examples:

Police Favor:
- Release arrested agent.
- Reduce Heat.
- Improve mission success.
- Delay investigation.
- Gain access to restricted areas.

The goal is to create meaningful spending decisions rather than single-purpose resources.

**Purpose**

- Increase strategic depth.
- Improve resource competition.
- Reduce resource bloat.

---

### Event-Driven Interaction

Institutions should not require constant management.

Preferred approach:

- Institutions operate mostly in the background.
- Significant situations appear periodically.
- Players react when opportunities or threats arise.

Examples:

- Major investigation.
- Corruption scandal.
- Election cycle.
- Court case.

**Purpose**

- Reduce maintenance gameplay.
- Keep institutions impactful without becoming repetitive.
- Focus attention on meaningful decisions.

---

## Current Direction

Institutions create problems and opportunities.

Leverage assets allow players to manipulate those situations.

The focus is on:

Problem → Decision → Outcome

Rather than:

Influence Meter → Bonus

---

## Open Questions

- How many institutions should exist?
- Are institutions global, district-based, or both?
- How often should institutions generate events or pressures?
- Should different leverage types have unique effects, or remain mechanically equivalent for now?
- Can institutions become major strategic actors without creating excessive complexity?
